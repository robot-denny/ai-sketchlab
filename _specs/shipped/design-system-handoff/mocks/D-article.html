What is ethics?

And why do most conversations about it collapse before they begin?

By Dennis Kardys· April 2, 2026· 8 min read

Most conversations about AI ethics fail in the same place: the second sentence. Someone says "ethics" and someone else hears "rules," and the thread is already unspooled. The two people aren't disagreeing about ethics. They're using the same word for two different things, and neither has noticed.

A working definition

Ethics is what you owe the people affected by your choices when you can't see them. That's the whole thing. Everything else — frameworks, principles, audits — is a scaffold around that one obligation. Scaffolds matter; they're how the obligation gets built out in practice. But they're not the obligation itself, and mistaking one for the other is how ethics conversations collapse.

Ethics is what you owe the people affected by your choices when you can't see them.

If that sentence feels unfinished, good. It should. Most of the hard work is in the second half — "the people affected by your choices when you can't see them" does most of the lifting, and the part we're worst at.

Written by Ella · inline note

I'd add: ethics is also what the model owes you. The reciprocal direction matters — and it's the one engineers usually forget. A system designed around one-way obligation produces one-way relationships.

Why the thread unspools

When someone hears "ethics" and translates it to "rules," they aren't misreading you — they're importing a whole history of the word's use in their field. For lawyers, ethics is compliance. For philosophers, it's a branch of inquiry. For engineers, too often, it's a checklist attached to a review meeting. None of those are wrong; they're all partial.

The useful move is not to correct the translation. It's to ask what your field's default translation costs you — what obligations get elided when ethics means "the rules we followed" instead of "the people we owed."
